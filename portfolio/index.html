Flagship agent · case study

The Undercut Engine

A competitive-intelligence agent for the enterprise CX / agentic-AI market. It reads the market twice a day, turns each competitor move into an attack angle plus a counter, and routes the play to the team that owns it — with an accuracy layer that would rather stay silent than be wrong.

Autonomous · running on cron since June 2026

From "here's some competitor news" → to a decision with an owner

Live in production
01 · The opportunity

B2B competitive intelligence usually dies in a graveyard of Slack links and stale decks. By the time a rival's move reaches someone who could act on it, it's old and un-owned. I reframed the problem: not "how do we track competitors," but "how do we turn every competitor move into a decision, assigned to an owner, within hours." That reframe is the whole product.

02 · Who it's for — the job to be done

One event, four different jobs. When a rival ships performance-based pricing, Sales needs a trap question for the next call, Product Marketing needs counter-messaging, Analyst Relations needs a validation angle, and Product needs a roadmap signal. The agent writes for all four off the same event — so intelligence lands as action, not as a newsletter nobody opens.

03 · The product in one move

Every signal is forced through the same shape — threat → counter → owner. Here's an actual pairing the agent produced:

◤ Competitor signal
Salesforce launched Agentforce Help Agent with performance-based pricing — a direct pull on enterprise budgets and a pricing-narrative land-grab.
◢ Our counter · routed to PMM + Sales
Lead with cost-per-outcome, determinism, and governance. Trap question for Sales: "When a prompt-built agent breaks a compliance step, do you find out at build time — or in production?"
04 · The product decisions — where the judgment lives
Decision 01

Precision over recall

Two verification gates plus an output-grounding audit that deletes any claim not backed by a verified, dated source. A wrong battlecard is worse than a missing one.

Traded: coverage, for trust the go-to-market teams can quote in front of a customer.
Decision 02

Decisions, not headlines

Refused to build "another AI newsletter." Every item must carry an attack angle, a counter, and a named team — or it doesn't ship.

Traded: volume, for the thing that actually changes what a team does Monday.
Decision 03

Config is the product surface

Watchlist, undercut levers, and alert triggers all live in one YAML a non-engineer edits. Strategy is tuned without touching code — policy separated from mechanism.

Traded: a slicker UI, for a strategist who can retarget the whole engine in five minutes.
Decision 04

Resilience is a feature

A 5-model fallback chain, retry-with-backoff, and 7-day dedup state. An unattended product that silently dies is worse than no product at all.

Traded: simplicity, for a system that still delivers when a free-tier model 503s at 2am.
Decision 05

Honesty guardrails

If the model didn't actually search the live web, the digest is banner-flagged "unverified" and breaking alerts are suppressed entirely. Trust beats coverage, structurally.

Traded: the appearance of always-on, for output the reader can rely on without checking.
Decision 06

Zero-marginal-cost sourcing

Fresh news comes from Google News RSS (free, no key), fed to the model to synthesize — instead of paying per search API call.

Traded: a premium data feed, for a product that runs indefinitely at near-zero cost.

06 · Outcomes & what I'd measure next

Shipped and operating unattended twice daily since June 2026, with a rolling archive of dated digests and a breaking-alert channel that stays quiet unless something real breaks. It runs on a living knowledge base of 13 battlecards built from internal decks plus fresh web research.

If I owned this as a funded product, I'd move past shipping-metrics to leading indicators of value: play-adoption rate (how often a routed play is actually used), time-from-event-to-action, and battlecard win-rate in contested deals — then feed those back to prioritize which levers the engine hunts for.

The through-line

One agent, three disciplines

This isn't a coding project that happens to touch strategy. It's a product decision, a market thesis, and a distribution system — each visible in the same artifact.

PM Product
  • Problem reframing — from "track competitors" to "produce owned decisions."
  • Jobs-to-be-done — one event written four ways for four teams.
  • Tradeoffs under ambiguity — precision over recall, documented, on purpose.
  • Ship & operate — it runs unattended, not in a demo tab.
STR Strategy
  • Market map — 13 rivals across four archetypes, from CX-native startups to big-tech platforms.
  • Positioning wedges — analyst validation, determinism, cost-per-outcome.
  • War-gaming — every move met with a counter and a trap question.
  • Signal triage — high / med / low threat, so attention goes where it pays.
GRW Growth
  • Distribution by design — cadence, channels, and a last-mile PDF, not just generation.
  • Leverage — one system serves four teams at zero added effort.
  • Near-zero marginal cost — free sourcing, so it scales indefinitely.
  • Metric instinct — a clear view of the leading indicators worth chasing next.
